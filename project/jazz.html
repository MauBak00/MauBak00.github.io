Overview

Starting in the later 19th century, African Americans in the Southern United States were influenced by the music created by slaves, as well as ragtime and the blues. They merged African, American, and European musical elements to create the earliest forms of jazz. Since then, jazz has surpassed racial boundaries and been established as a major musical form. Throughout its history, jazz has evolved in response to the social and musical conditions of its time. While very different musical styles have come to be included under the genre of jazz, they all tend to be distinguished by several shared features. Key among these are the use of swing and polyrhythms, call and response patterns, blue notes, and improvization.





The Atlantic slave trade brought immense suffering to millions, while laying the foundations for what would later become jazz.




Slave Music

Between the 17th to the 19th centuries, 400,000 slaves were forcibly brought to the United States, mostly from West Africa. Taken from their homes and families and shoved into the terrible hardships of slavery, many slaves sought to retain their musical traditions as a means of preserving their identities and making their suffering more bearable. Common forms of slaves' musical expression included work songs, spirituals, and ring shouts.

Ragtime

After the emancipation of Southern slaves in the Reconstruction era, more blacks were able to pursue careers as musicians. At the same time, pianos were becoming more widely used. Ragtime, characterized by emphatically syncopated rhythms, developed from African Americans who were pianists for popular entertainment venues of the time. The techniques of ragtime strongly influenced those of early jazz pianists.

In 1899, pianist and composer Scott Joplin published Maple Leaf Rag, perhaps the most well-known ragtime song.




Blues

The blues, which developed in the American South, included African as well us uniquely American musical elements, and was a major influence on jazz. Songs in this genre expressed the pain and grief of the late 19th century, one known for economic depression and virulent racism. While the earliest blues musicians were singers who accompoanied themselves with a guitar, later female vocalists with brass bands enjoyed popular success. The blues was characterized by a distinctive chord progression, the use of blue notes, instrumental improvization, and vocal lines that resembled those sung by slaves.

Jazz begun in New Orleans, blending African, European, North American, and Latin musical elements while developing new ones. Also known as Dixieland, early New Orleans jazz was characterized by large brass-based bands, a 2-beat "oom-pa" rhythm, a bright and energetic feel, and collective improvization that included the impromptu creation of melodic embellishment and counter-melodies.

Jazz Sub-Genres

Many different styles have developed from this musical foundation. They are described in the following table.



Sub-Genre Early Development Characteristics Example
Swing 1920's and 1930's Big bands with large horn section, extensive individual improvisational solos, swing rhythm, danceable and bright, gained nationwide popularity Louis Armstrong's "Big Butter & Egg Man"
Bebop Early 1940's More complex music by next generation of jazz musicians, meant for listening instead of dancing, fast tempos, dissonance, complex rhythms and harmonies Charlie Parker's "Anthropology"
Cool Jazz Late 1940's, 1950's Moved away from speed and complexity of bebop, using more relaxed speeds and lighter tones, reflecting the calmer West Coast lifestyle Miles Davis's Birth of the Cool
Hard Bop Mid-1950's Extension of bebop, with greater influence from rhythm and blues, which was gaining popularity at the time Art Blakey & The Jazz Messengers' Moanin'
Modal Jazz Late 1950's Used simple harmonies, instead emphasizing melodic improvisation based on modes Miles Davis's Kind of Blue
Latin Jazz Varied Jazz featuring rhythms, styles, and instrumentation from Cuba, Brazil, and other parts of Latin America Tito Puente's Vaya Puente
Jazz Fusion Late 1960's and 1970's Merging of jazz with other musical genres, including rock, psychedelic, and funk music. Miles Davis's Bitches Brew
